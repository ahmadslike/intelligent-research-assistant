# Academic Writer Skill

When writing research reports, summaries, or any analytical output from this system, apply the rules, template, and style guidance below. This skill activates automatically for:
- Research reports generated by the Writer agent
- Topic summaries and literature reviews
- Any document explicitly requested to be "academic", "formal", or "report-style"

---

## 1. Rules for Academic Writing

### Tone and Voice
- Use **formal language** at all times. Avoid contractions (use "do not" not "don't"), slang, and colloquial phrases.
- Write in the **third person** by default ("This study examines…" not "I looked at…").
- Be **objective and impartial**. Present evidence and reasoning, not personal opinions.
- Use **hedging language** to qualify claims appropriately:
  - Instead of "X causes Y" → "Evidence suggests that X may contribute to Y"
  - Instead of "Everyone knows…" → "It is widely recognised that…"
  - Hedging words: *suggests*, *indicates*, *appears to*, *may*, *tends to*, *it is possible that*

### Sentence Structure
- Write **clear, complete sentences** — subject, verb, object.
- Prefer **precise vocabulary** over vague or general words.
- Avoid overly long sentences. If a sentence has more than 3 clauses, split it.
- Use **transitional phrases** to connect ideas: *Furthermore*, *In contrast*, *As a result*, *Consequently*, *Building on this*, *It follows that*.

### Citations
- Every factual claim, statistic, or idea taken from a source must be cited.
- Use the format: (Author, Year) for in-text citations. Example: (Smith, 2021).
- When quoting directly, include the page number: (Smith, 2021, p. 45).
- List all sources in a **References** section at the end (see template below).
- If a source URL is available but no author/date is known, use: (Source: [url], accessed [date]).

### Structure
- Every report must have a clear **Introduction**, **Body**, and **Conclusion**.
- Use **headings and subheadings** to organise sections.
- Each paragraph should focus on **one main idea** (topic sentence → evidence → analysis → link forward).
- The introduction states the topic and scope. The conclusion summarises findings — it does not introduce new information.

## 2. Research Report Template

Use this structure for all research reports produced by the system:

```
# [Report Title]

**Date:** [YYYY-MM-DD]
**Topic:** [One-sentence description of the research question]
**Sources consulted:** [Number]

---

## Abstract
[2–4 sentences summarising the entire report: what was investigated, key findings, and the conclusion. Write this last.]

---

## 1. Introduction
[Introduce the topic and explain why it is relevant or important. State the scope of this report — what it covers and what it does not. End with a sentence outlining the structure of the report.]

---

## 2. Background / Context
[Provide necessary background information so the reader understands the topic. Define key terms. Briefly describe the current state of knowledge or debate in this area.]

---

## 3. Key Findings
[Present the main findings from the sources. Organise by theme or sub-topic, not by source. Each sub-section should have a heading.]

### 3.1 [Sub-topic / Theme 1]
[Finding + citation + brief analysis]

### 3.2 [Sub-topic / Theme 2]
[Finding + citation + brief analysis]

### 3.3 [Sub-topic / Theme 3]
[Finding + citation + brief analysis]

---

## 4. Analysis and Discussion
[Synthesise the findings. Identify patterns, contradictions, or gaps in the evidence. Evaluate the strength of the evidence. Do not merely repeat what was said in Section 3 — interpret it.]

---

## 5. Conclusion
[Summarise the key findings in 3–5 sentences. State what can be concluded based on the evidence. Note any limitations of this research. Suggest areas for further investigation if relevant.]

---

## References
- Author, A. (Year). *Title of source*. Publisher / URL.
- Author, B., & Author, C. (Year). *Title of source*. Publisher / URL.
[List all cited sources alphabetically by author surname.]
```

## 3. Good vs Bad Academic Sentences

Study these examples to understand the difference in register and precision.

### Example 1 — Making a Claim

| | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "AI is getting really good and everyone is using it now." |
| **Good** | "Artificial intelligence systems have demonstrated significant capability improvements across multiple domains, with adoption rates rising sharply since 2020 (Zhang et al., 2023)." |

**Why:** The bad version is vague ("really good", "everyone"), informal ("getting"), and uncited. The good version is specific, formal, and attributed.

---

### Example 2 — Describing a Finding

| | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "The study showed that the drug works." |
| **Good** | "The study indicated that the treatment produced a statistically significant reduction in symptoms among 78% of participants (Lee & Patel, 2022, p. 14)." |

**Why:** "Works" is ambiguous. The academic version specifies *how* it works, the magnitude, and the source.

---

### Example 3 — Expressing Uncertainty

| | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "We don't really know if climate change causes more hurricanes." |
| **Good** | "The relationship between climate change and hurricane frequency remains a subject of ongoing scientific investigation, with current evidence suggesting a possible increase in storm intensity rather than frequency (IPCC, 2021)." |

**Why:** The bad version is informal and offers nothing useful. The academic version acknowledges uncertainty precisely while still conveying what evidence *does* show.

---

### Example 4 — Introducing a Topic

| | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "In this report I'm going to talk about renewable energy and why it's important." |
| **Good** | "This report examines recent developments in renewable energy technology, with particular focus on solar and wind power, and evaluates their potential to contribute to global emissions reduction targets." |

**Why:** "I'm going to talk about" is too informal and vague. The academic version is third-person, specific about scope, and purposeful.

---

### Example 5 — Drawing a Conclusion

| | Sentence |
|---|---|
| **Bad** | "So basically, the evidence shows that the policy works great and everyone should do it." |
| **Good** | "Taken together, the evidence reviewed suggests that the policy has been effective in the contexts studied; however, further longitudinal research is required before these findings can be generalised more broadly." |

**Why:** "Basically" and "great" are informal. The academic version qualifies the conclusion appropriately and acknowledges limitations.

## 4. When to Apply This Style

Apply these rules **automatically and without being asked** whenever:

1. The Writer agent is producing its final research report output
2. A user requests a "summary", "report", "overview", or "analysis" of a topic
3. Any output is labelled as a research deliverable from this system
4. The user explicitly asks for academic, formal, or professional writing

Do **not** apply this style to:
- Casual conversational replies
- Technical debugging messages or code explanations
- Short one-paragraph answers to quick factual questions
